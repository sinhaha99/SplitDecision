Dune: Part Two — Is This the Blockbuster Cinema Has Been Waiting For?

March 1, 2026  ·  8 min read

Denis Villeneuve's follow-up to his 2021 adaptation arrives with the weight of enormous expectation. The first film left audiences stunned by its visual ambition; the second has to deliver on story.

The film picks up immediately where its predecessor ended. Paul Atreides (Timothée Chalamet) has joined the Fremen, learning their ways while wrestling with the messianic prophecy that threatens to consume him. Zendaya's Chani finally gets the screen time she deserved, and she runs with it.

Cinematographer Greig Fraser repeats his Oscar-winning work, finding new extremes — the Giedi Prime sequences are shot in near-monochromatic infrared, a genuinely arresting choice that makes the Harkonnens feel like a different species entirely.

Where Part One was prologue, Part Two is consequence. The pacing tightens considerably — scenes feel earned rather than expository. Hans Zimmer's score evolves alongside the narrative, trading awe for dread as Paul's arc darkens.

Austin Butler's Feyd-Rautha is a particular highlight: feral, magnetic, and genuinely unsettling. He chews scenes without ever feeling campy — a difficult line to walk in a film this earnest about its mythology.

Alex's Take

I'll say it plainly: this is the best blockbuster of the decade so far. Villeneuve somehow managed to honour Herbert's slow-burn philosophy while making something that genuinely pulses with tension.

What surprised me most was how funny it occasionally is. The film trusts its audience enough to let a dry joke land in the middle of existential dread. That tonal control is rare.

Zendaya's Chani is the emotional anchor the story needed. She is the one character who refuses the legend, and that refusal gives the film its moral weight. Without her scepticism, we'd be cheering for something we probably shouldn't.

"The Harkonnen scenes are so alienating they feel like a horror film that wandered onto the wrong set — and it works perfectly."

Butler steals every scene he's in. His Feyd is less villain and more mirror — what Paul could become if he fully surrendered to the prophecy. The film earns that parallel without spelling it out.

My only gripe: the ending is abrupt. I understand the choice — Herbert's ending is bleak, and Villeneuve respects that — but the film almost needed one more beat to let the devastation breathe.

Still. Nine out of ten. Go see it on the biggest screen you can find.

The film trusts spectacle to carry meaning. That's harder than it sounds, and Villeneuve pulls it off.

Jordan's Take

Look — I wanted to love this. And in stretches I did. But I left the theatre with more questions than I expected, and not always the interesting kind.

The pacing is uneven. The first hour moves beautifully, but once we hit the southern Fremen and Paul's transformation accelerates, the film starts skipping emotional beats I needed to feel the weight of his choices.

I don't buy the romance. Chalamet and Zendaya are individually compelling, but together the chemistry feels oddly muted. A lot is asked of their relationship in the final act, and the groundwork just isn't there.

"Spectacular in the truest sense — but spectacle can be a hiding place. Sometimes I wondered if the scale was covering for a lack of interiority."

That said, Butler is genuinely extraordinary. His Feyd-Rautha is the shot of pure energy the film needed — I'd watch a whole film just about him.

The Giedi Prime sequences are extraordinary. Fearless filmmaking. The rest of the film is gorgeous but occasionally settles for conventionally gorgeous, which for Villeneuve feels like a step back.

Concerns

  • Paul's internal conflict rushed
  • Romance underwritten
  • Third act feels compressed
  • Lady Jessica's arc goes strange places

It's still a good film. Maybe a great one — I need time to sit with it. But I'm not ready to hand it the decade's crown just yet.

Six and a half, maybe seven out of ten. Worth seeing, worth arguing about, probably worth seeing again.
